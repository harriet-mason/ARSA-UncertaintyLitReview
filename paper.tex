\begin{abstract}
Uncertainty visualisation is quickly becoming a hot topic in information
visualisation. Existing reviews in the field take the definition and
purpose of an uncertainty visualisation to be self evident which results
in a large amout of conflicting information. This conflict largely stems
from a conflation between uncertainty visualisations designed for
decision making and those designed to prevent false conclusions. We coin
the term ``signal suppression'' to describe a visualisation that is
designed for preventing false conclusions, as the approach demands that
the signal (i.e.~the collective take away of the estimates) is
suppressed by the noise (i.e.~the variance on those estimates). We argue
that the current standards in visualisation suggest that uncertainty
visualisations designed for decision making should not be considered
uncertainty visualisations at all. Therefore, future work should focus
on signal suppression. Effective signal suppression requires us to
communicate the signal and the noise as a single ``validity of signal''
variable, and doing so proves to be difficult with current methods. We
illustrate current approaches to uncertainty visualisation by showing
how they would change the visual apprearance of a choropleth map. These
maps allow us to see why some methods succeed at signal suppression,
while others fall short. Evaluating visualisations on how well they
perform signal suppression also proves to be difficult, as it involves
measuring the effect of noise, a variable we typically try to ignore. We
suggest authors use qualitative studies or compare uncertainty
visualisations to the relevant hypothesis tests.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{introduction}

The phrase ``uncertainty visualisation'' can sometimes feel like a bit
of an oxymoron. Uncertainty is often discussed as though it is an
omnipresent spectre, touching every stage of our analysis. The fact that
nobody really knows what it is, is either directly stated by authors who
work in the field \citep{Spiegelhalter2017, Griethe2006} or implied by
describing a myriad of things uncertainty could be without a concrete
definition \citep{Kinkeldey2014, Hullman2016}. Contrary to the murky
waters of uncertainty, visualisations illuminates and unveils the
unknowns in our data. This is illustrated with datasets such as
Anscombe's quartet \citep{anscombe} or the Datasaurus Dozen
\citep{datasaurus, datasaurpkg} where visual statistics reveal elements
of the data that are hidden to the typical summary statistics. This
leads us to the conclusions that uncertainty visualisation is about
revealing the unknown and shining a light on the more questionable parts
of our analysis.

This sentiment is regularly echoed by the literature where we see
authors express that visualising uncertainty is necessary for
transparency and improved decision making \citep{uncertchap2022}.
Failing to incorporate the uncertain information in an analysis will
lead to research being undermined as evidenced by a 6-month survey of
anti-mask groups on Facebook during the COVID-19 pandemic that showed
anti-maskers made persuasive arguments by exploiting inherrent
uncertainty that was ignored by pro-maskers \citep{Lee2021}.
Additionally, failing to present uncertain information can lead to poor
estimation of failure probabilities \citep{Kiureghian2009}, and poorer
decision making both experimentally
\citep{Joslyn2012, Savelli2013, Kay2016, Fernandes2018} and in practice
\citep{Al-Kassab2014}.

Given the importance of uncertainty visualisation, it would be nice if
the field had some overarching guidelines, but this turns out to be
easier asked for, than done. Rather than providing an overarching,
cohesive narrative of the field, it is far more common to see a review
paper lament the \emph{difficulties} faced when trying to extract any
cohesive insights. \citet{Kinkeldey2014} found most experimental methods
to be ad hoc, with no commonly agreed upon methodology, formalisations,
or greater goal of describing general principals. \citet{Hullman2016}
noticed there is a serious noise issue in the field, with noise from
participants misunderstanding visualisations, misinterpreting questions,
and incorrectly applying heuristics all overwhelming any information we
can glean from studies. \citet{MacEachren2005} identified so much
contradicting evidence that they were able to spend an entire page
discussing the contradictions around a question as simple as ``Should I
map uncertainty to colour hue?''. \citet{Griethe2006} did not identify
common themes, but instead listed the findings and opinions of a
collection of papers. \citet{uncertchap2022} was able to synthesise some
results cognative effects that arise in uncertainty visualisation
experiments, however these effects were each discussed in isolation as a
list of considerations an author might make rather than an overarching
theory of rules for effective uncertainty visualisation. While these
reviews are thorough in scope, none discuss exactly what we are trying
to do hwen visualising uncertainty, or how the existing literature
contribute to the broader goal of the field. The problem faced by the
literature is easily summarised with a famous quote by Henri Poincaré.

\begin{quote}
``Science is built up of facts, as a house is built of stones; but an
accumulation of facts is no more a science than a heap of stones is a
house.'' - Henri Poincaré (1905)
\end{quote}

That is to say, despite the wealth of reviews, the field of uncertainty
visualisation remains a heap of stones. This review attempts to address
this issue by offering a novel perspective on the uncertainty
visualisation problem. That is, we are going to use the wealth of
established stone to construct the foundations on which we can build a
house.

\section{The purpose of uncertainty
visualisation}\label{the-purpose-of-uncertainty-visualisation}

Reading the uncertainty visualisation literature, we can become turned
around as it often feels like we are reading several independent
conversations at once. Largely because, we are.

Early mentions of uncertainty visualisation start to spring up in the
late 1980s and early 1990s across several different fields
\citep{Ibrekk1987, MacEachren1992}, each with their own motivation for
the work, with statistics often dappling in three. The work in computer
science is motivated by issues in the publics perception of random
variables, with the hope that visualisations would give laypeople the
ability to extract important information from graphical representations
\citep{Ibrekk1987}. This field views uncertainty visualisation as a
human perception problem, where researchers often compare box plots,
density plots, histograms, and other visual representations of a
distribution to see which approach allows people to best extract means,
probabilities, variances, etc. The methods discussed by
psychology/economics also have similar foundations in the publics
understanding of randomness, as they are concerned with using
visualisations to help mitigate psychological bias that comes up when
you try to communicate risk, a problem of concern since the early 1980s
\citep{Spiegelhalter2017}. These approaches often utilise pictographs
and stress the importance of correct framing of the probabilities.
Finally, around the early 1990s, discussions of uncertainty pop up in
the cartography literature motivated by the inherent uncertainty in the
measurements or estimates they are trying to visualise within the
constrained visual environment of maps \citep{MacEachren1992, Carr1992}.
This area views uncertainty visualisation as a problem in high
dimensional visualisation, where uncertainty is just another variable
that needs to be integrated into the visualisation
\citep{Griethe2006, geointerviews}. As the field trudges on these
distinct motivations bleed together and are now treated interchangeably
as ``uncertainty visualisation''. We see computer science and psychology
combine to form the research on ``decision making'' where researchers
see which distribution representation elicits the best decisions and
best mitigate psychological bias
\citep{Joslyn2012, Savelli2013, Kay2016, Fernandes2018}. We see
geoscience and computer science combine to evaluate map representations
based on how well viewers can extract statistics about the distribution
\citep{Maceachren2012, Correll2018, Ndlovu2023}.

This all begs the question, if theses fields do not agree on the purpose
of uncertaitny visualisation, why have they intermixed so much that the
literature treats them as interchangeable? There seems to be a single
common thread that connects these approaches, that comes up time and
time again across all subfields, and that is the belief that uncertainty
visualisation is in pursuit of \emph{transparency}. We see it comes up
when authors claim that failing to include uncertainty is akin to fraud
or lying \citep{Hullman2020a, Manski2020}. We see it when authors assert
that uncertainty communicates the legitimacy (or illegitimacy) of the
conclusion drawn from visual inference
\citep{Correll2014, Kale2018, Griethe2006}. We see it when authors say
uncertainty visualisations should communicate a degree of confidence
\citep{Boukhelifa2012} or validity \citep{Hullman2020a, Griethe2006} in
our conclusions. When authors suggest uncertainty visualisation should
``guide, qualify, or soften our judgments of uncertain data''
\citep{Leland2005}. When authors assert uncertainty visualisation should
communivate a proportional level of trust in our data that is more
detailed than the binary results of a hypothesis test
\citep{Correll2014, Correll2018}. Combining these statements together,
there is one glaring through line, and it is the assertion that
uncertainty visualisation should act as some kind of visual hypothesis
test where conclusions are visible when they are backed up by data, and
invisible when they are not.

Therefore, the one fundamental motivator of the field is the idea that
uncertainty visualisation should act as a ``statistical hedge'' for any
inference we make using the graphic, and the other goals. Since the
purpose of a visualisation is to give a quick gist of the information
\citep{Spiegelhalter2017}, this hedging should be communicated
immediately through the graphic without the need for complicated mental
calculations. Therefore, an effective uncertainty visualisation should
not just ``show'' uncertainty, untrustworthy conclusions should
\emph{not be visible}. If we refer to the conclusion we draw from a
graphic to be its signal and the uncertainty that makes this signal
harder to identify as the ``noise'', we can summarise the above
information into three key requirements. A good uncertainty
visualisation needs to:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi})}
\tightlist
\item
  Reinforce justified signals to encourage confidence in results
\item
  Hide signals that are overwhelmed by noise to prevent unjustified
  conclusions
\item
  Perform tasks 1) and 2) in a way that is proportional to the level of
  confidence in those conclusions.
\end{enumerate}

Usually visualisations that are unconcerned with uncertainty have no
issue showing justified signals, but struggle with the display of
unjustified signals. Therefore, we call this approach to uncertainty
visualisation as ``signal-suppression'' since it primarily
differentiates itself from from the normal ``noiseless'' visualisation
approach through criteria (2). This is the main criteria we will use to
assess uncertainty visualisations as we disentangle which approaches
integrate uncertainty as signal, and which integrate uncertainty as
noise.

\section{Current Approaches}\label{current-approaches}

Even if transparency is the key pursuit of the field, most
visualisations that are developed are developed with reference to their
specific problem. That is, they are trying to come up with flexible
distribution representations or new mappings that uncertainty could be
mapped to in an existing graphic. These goals are not necessarily in
conflict with the goal of transparency, so we will look at the existing
approaches developed in the field and assess which successfully act as a
statistical hedge, and discuss why, or why not, certain approaches do,
or do not, work.

\subsection{Ignoring uncertainty}\label{ignoring-uncertainty}

The most common way uncertainty is integrated into a visualisation is
actually to not do it at all. A survey conducted by \citet{Hullman2020a}
found that very few authors actually include uncertainty in their
visualisations, with only a quarter of respondents included uncertainty
in 50\% or more of their visualisations. Part of the abstinence of
uncertainty visualisation comes from the fact that authors think that
the plots will be overwhelming or they are uncertain how to calculate
the uncertainty \citep{Hullman2020a}. This is not entirely unreasonable,
given that even visualisation authors themselves seem to be in conflict
about what exactly uncertainty is, and it is hard to assert people are
ignoring it if we don't even know what it is they are ignoring.

\subsubsection{What is uncertainty?}\label{what-is-uncertainty}

Describing what uncertainty actually is, is surprisingly hard. Most
authors simply avoid the problem and describe the characteristics of
uncertainty, of which there are plenty. Examples of these distinctions
include whether or not the uncertainty is:

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  Due to true randomness or a lack of knowledge
  \citep{Begg2014, Spiegelhalter2017, Gustafson2019, uncertchap2022, Hullman2016, utypo}
\item
  Quantifiable or unquantifiable
  \citep{Spiegelhalter2017, utypo, uncertchap2022}
\item
  Scientific or human \citep{Benjamin2018, Gustafson2019}
\item
  Systematic or random (variance) \citep{Sanyal2009}
\item
  Statistical or bounded \citep{Gschwandtnei2016, Olston2002}
\item
  In accuracy or precision
  \citep{Griethe2006, Benjamin2018, Hullman2016}
\end{itemize}

We could go on, there are enough taxonomies and typologies of
uncertainty to fill several papers. It is the most common approach
authors take to provide a definition, but the sheer volume of them makes
it hard to see the method as anything other than a distinction without a
difference, at least for our purposes. The distinctions are often
accurate, but have little, if any, impact on how we should visualise,
quantify, or discuss the uncertainty, bloating the literature and
offering little in terms of genuine insights.

Rather than trying to define uncertainty by looking at the myriad ways
in which it \emph{does} appear in an analysis, we may find it easier to
look at where it \emph{does not}. Descriptive statistics describe our
sample as it is and summarises large data down into an easy to swallow
format. Descriptive statistics are not seen as the primary goal of
modern statistics, however, this was not always the case. In 19th
century England, \emph{positivism} was the popular philosophical
approach to science (positivists included famous statisticians such as
Francis Galton and Karl Pearson). Practitioners of the approach believed
statistics ended with descriptive statistics as science must be based on
actual experience and observations \citep{Otsuka2023}. In order to make
statements about population statistics, future values, or new
observations we need to perform inference, which requires the assumption
of the ``uniformity of nature'', that is, we need to assume that
unobserved phenomena should be similar to observed phenomena
\citep{Otsuka2023}. Positivists believed referencing the unobservable
was bad science. In other words, these scientists embraced descriptive
statistics due to the inherent certainty that came with them. Since
uncertainty is non-existent in descriptive statistics, it is clear that
uncertainty is a by-product of inference. Looking at the previous
classifications, we can see that they either offer information on what
type of distribution to use (e.g.~precision and bounded uncertainty are
uniform distributions) or they offer suggestions in where to look to
quantify the uncertainty (e.g.~stage of the analysis, source of
uncertainty, lack of knowledge, etc). These taxonomies likely describe
what uncertainty is like or similar to because uncertainty is
fundamentally statistical inference the cost of statistical inference.

The relationship between inference and uncertainty frequently appears in
the literature and is one of the most consistent comments made, but it
is often reffered to as a dependence on the ``goals'' of our analysis
rather than inferential statistics. This has been known in some form or
another since the earliest work on the subject, with \citet{Ibrekk1987}
finding that ``The performance of a display depends upon the information
that a subject is trying to extract.'' \citet{Meng2014} commented what
is kept as data and what is tossed away is determined by the motivation
of an analysis and what was previously noise can be shown to become
signal depending on the question we seek to answer.\\
\citet{Otsuka2023} suggested that the process of observing data to
perform statistics is largely dependent on our goals, because the
process of boiling real world entities down into probabilistic objects
(or ``probabilistic kind'' as he puts it) depends on the relationship we
seek to identify with our data. \citet{Wallsten1997} argue that the best
method for evaluating or combining subjective probabilities depends on
the uncertainty the decision maker wants to represent and why it
matters. \citet{Fischhoff2014} looks at uncertainty visualisation for
decision making decides that we should have different ways of
communicating uncertainty based off what the user is supposed to do with
it.\\
\citet{Spiegelhalter2017} noted we ``cannot assess the quality of risk
communication unless the objectives are clear''. \citet{geointerviews}
asserted that uncertainty is context-dependent, and whether or not it is
a source of doubt depends on the context. The communicating of this
repeated point is clear, the role of signal and noise is not set in
stone, it is flexible and can easily change. The only constant is that
signal is our inferential statistic, and noise is the distribution of
outcomes around that signal.

As a uncertainty is by product of statistical inference, it would make
sense to simply quantify it as a distribution. Suggesting distributions
as a representation of uncertainty is not new, \citet{Kay2023}
originally suggested thinking about uncertainty visualisations with
distribution inputs, to replace the commonly used mean and standard
deviation, removing the assumption of Gaussian distribution. Taking this
point a step further we suggest that a distribution is sufficient to
characterise \emph{any} uncertainty, no matter how intangible it is.
This is in contrast to the assertions made by some authors, who believe
abstract notions of uncertainty, such as credibility
\citep{Thomson2005}, forecaster confidence \citep{Padilla2021}, or
uncertainty about uncertainty \citep{Hadjimichael2024}, are too complex
to be quantified. This is simply untrue, as abstract concepts such as
human belief or credibility are regularly quantified by Bayesians and
hierarchical models are often used for uncertainty about uncertainty.
Since uncertainty is always a by-product of statistical inference, it
should always be possible to quantify it using a distribution.

\subsubsection{Example: visualising
estimates}\label{example-visualising-estimates}

If visualising uncertainty is fundamentally a scenario where we are
visualising a set of random variables what does ``ignoring'' uncertainty
look like? Figure~\ref{fig-ignore} shows six different plots, a high and
low uncertainty case for three different plots, all of which simply
visualise the central mean of the distribution, which is what the
uncertainty visualisation literature typically means when they say a
method ``ignores'' uncertainty. Plots (a) and (b) show the line plot
from a simple linear regression on the \texttt{mtcars} data, the
regression in (a) has been done on the full dataset, and therefore has
low uncertainty, while (b) was done on a sample of three points and has
very high uncertainty. Plots (c) and (d) show a choropleth map, with
county in Iowa being coloured by a simulated measured temperature, with
plot (c) depicting a case where the measurement error is low, while plot
(d) depictes a case where the measurement error is high. Finally, plots
(e) and (f) depict a set of five groups of univariate variables we are
looking to compare, where (e) depicts a case where the group variance is
high (f) shows a case where the group variance is low. In all plots, we
can only choose a single value to represent the distribution, so we opt
for the average of all these distributions. In all plots, the variance
in the plots in the top row is significantly different to the plots in
the bottom row, so much so, that the trend should actually disappear.
Can you see a difference between the plots in the top row versus the
bottom row? Is the strength of the trend communicated through the
visualisation? The answer to both of these questions is no as the high
and low variance cases are identical.

\begin{figure}

\centering{

\includegraphics{paper_files/figure-pdf/fig-ignore-1.pdf}

}

\caption{\label{fig-ignore}Six different plots, each depicting a high
and low uncertainty case for three plots commonly seen in discussions
around uncertainty visualisation. The plots are: (a) linear regression
with low uncertainty, (b) linear regression with high uncertainty, (c)
spatial trend with low uncertainty, (d) spatial trend with high

These plots ignore uncertainty and as such, do not perform signal
suppression. They don't even show a visual difference in the plot as the
uncertainty changes. We will use these plots as a touchstone for our
discussion on uncertainty visualisation, and return to them when we
discuss the approaches suggested in the literature, assessing the
approach on how well it hides spurious relationships, and reveals the
hidden variance structures in the data.

\subsection{Uncertainty as a
statistic}\label{uncertainty-as-a-statistic}

The most common approach to uncertainty visualisation (after just
ignoring it all together) is to swap out our original statistic for one
that shows uncertainty. The argument that an ``uncertainty
visualisation'' should include visualisations of probability or variance
is rooted in the decision making approach to the field, as research in
this form is typically motivated by the psychological heuristics
involved in interpreting uncertainty \citep{Hullman2019}. These days it
pops up in all sub fields of visualisation. We see it as an exceedance
probability map in geoscience \citep{Kuhnert2018, Lucchesi2021}, as an
icon array in risk communication \citep{Spiegelhalter2017}, or as a
summary plot in statistics \citep{Potter2010}. Some authors take this
approach because they explicitly believe uncertainty is a variable of
importance in of itself \citep{Blenkinsop2000}, others strattle the
line, asserting uncertainty is acting as signal and noise and should
fulfil both roles \citep{geointerviews}. This seems a little too easy,
can we just swap out the statistic for an ``uncertainty statistic'' and
get an ``uncertainty visualisation''? Is it that easy?

\subsubsection{Example: visualising
error}\label{example-visualising-error}

Figure~\ref{fig-statistic} depicts the six visualisations from
Figure~\ref{fig-ignore}, except the original central value estimate has
been replaced with an uncertainty statistic. Visually these plots are
identical with the visualisations remaining as a line plot, a choropleth
map, and a scatter plot, with the change being in the meaning behind the
plot rather than the visual appearance. Plots (a) and (b) are the most
absurd examples of this visualisation approach, as replacing the
estimated slope and intercept of a line with the standard error of said
estimate produces a plot that is inherently meaningless and is obviously
completely unrelated to the data. This will not always be the case, as
plots (c) and (d) are exceedance probability maps, real plots used in
the literature to depict the probability of \(P(X>27)\) for the
temperature of each county. Plots (e) and (f) simply show a scatter plot
that depicts the variance of the groups instead of the estimate. The
trend is no longer visible in the regression or the group comparison,
but that is because the meaning of the plot has fundamentally changed,
while the trend in the exceed probability map is still visible, although
the meaning of this plot is also different.

\begin{figure}

\centering{

\subsubsection{What is an uncertainty
visualisation?}\label{what-is-an-uncertainty-visualisation}

When we refer to these graphics as ``uncertainty visualisations'' we are
classifying visualisations by the data they display, not their visual
features, even though graphics have never been defined by their input
data. A scatter plot that compares mean and a scatter plot that compares
variances are both scatter plots. Given that there is no special class
of visualisation for \emph{other} statistics (such the median or
maximum) there is no reason to assume visualisations that simply depict
a variance, error, or probability to be special.

The question about what is, or is not, an uncertainty visualisation
seems to be one of the most pervasive divides in the literature. For
example \citet{Leland2005} mentions that popular graphics, such as pie
charts and bar charts omit uncertainty, and \citet{Wickham2011} suggests
their product plot framework, which includes histograms and bar charts,
should be extended to include uncertainty. However, pie charts, bar
charts and histograms have all been used as a significant number of
experiments as examples of an ``uncertainty visualisation''
\citep{Ibrekk1987, Olston2002, Zhao2023, Hofmann2012}. What is going on
here? Well, this problem seems to go back to the motivation of the field
we discussed earlier. If you believe uncertainty visualisation is about
communicating risks or random variables, uncertainty visualisations are
just visualisations of ``uncertainty statistics'' or distributions. On
the other hand you believe uncertainty visualisation is a dimensionality
problem, then uncertainty visualisation is a function applied to an
existing graphic that somehow adds the uncertainty in. The former has no
limitation on the visual appearance of an ``uncertainty visualisation'',
allowing pie charts, bar charts or histograms, so long as the graphic is
visualising ``uncertainty'', while the latter believes uncertainty
visualisations only exist in relation to some ``normal'' visualisation.

The fundamental problem with the statistic approach is that the
uncertainty in the plot isn't acting as noise, it is acting as signal.
This is because the ``uncertainty statistic'' has become the statistic
we are drawing inference on, which mean our new statistic \emph{also has
uncertainty attached to it}, and therefore also have it's own
uncertainty visualisation. Fundamentally, we have simply changed our
inferential statistic, and in doing so, swapped the role of signal and
noise. Of course, there is nothing wrong with explicitly visualising
variance, error, bias, or any other statistic used to depict uncertainty
as a signal. Just like any other statistic, these metrics provide
important and useful information for analysis and decisions. However,
this approach means that everything is an uncertainty visualisation, and
if everything is an uncertainty visualisation, nothing is. Additionally,
there is no interesting visual challenge associated with these graphics
as they do not require any special visualisation techniques. The
uncertainty in these graphics are acting as a signal variable, and they
should be visualised using existing principles of graphic design

\subsection{Uncertainty as a variable}\label{uncertainty-as-a-variable}

If we need to include uncertainty in a plot, the enxt This approach
treats uncertainty as a variable and is quite common, with many authors,
across computer science \citep{Kinkeldey2014}, cartogophy
\citep{MacEachren2005}, and statistical graphics \citep{Leland2005}
implicitly treating this ``additional aesthetic'' approach as the
\emph{only} method. We often see authors take this approach to
uncertainty visualisation, not because we recognise the role in
inference in our analysis, but rather because it is believed that
displaying the features of one distribution across multiple plots makes
the information hard to combine and results in some details (such as the
estimate error) being completely ignored {[}\citet{moritz2017trust};
Correll2018{]}.

\subsubsection{Example: mapping independent

the plot. \citet{Kinkeldey2014} identified a split between intrinsic
plots, where we map uncertainty to the colour or size of the geometric
object of our estimate, and extrinsic plots where uncertainty is mapped
to a separate geometric object glyphs or error bars. A similar
distinction was made by \citet{uncertchap2022} who classified
uncertainty visualisations as graphical annotations (extrinsic), and
probability mapped to a visual encoding channel (intrinsic), or a hybrid
of the two. While these discussions to draw attention to how
``integrated'' the uncertainty is in the visualisation, it is unclear if
this ``intrinsic'' plot

Figure~\ref{fig-variable} depicts the six visualisations
Figure~\ref{fig-ignore} except we have also mapped uncertainty to a
spare aesthetic in the plot. The integrable approach is shown in plots
(a) and (b) we have mapped the standard error of the slope to the width
of the line, and the standard error of the intercept to the
transparency. A bivariate map is shown in plots (c) and (d) and it
offers a slight divergence from the intrinsic/extrinsic approach as it

visualising two independent signals, rather than a signal and it's
noise. This method clearly is not visualising uncertainty as a ``single
integrated uncertain value'' as \citet{Kinkeldey2014} would put it. The
reality is, based on our discussion on inferential statistics,
uncertainty \emph{isn't} a separate variable, it is an component of the
random variable that is indistinguishable from the random variable
itself. It is worthwhile to interrogate why this occurs to see if we can
move towards a version of this plot where we are able to communicate
signal and noise as a single signal.

\subsubsection{How to visualise a ``single integrated uncertain
value''}\label{how-to-visualise-a-single-integrated-uncertain-value}

Typically, when making visualisations, we want the visual channels to be
separable, that is, we don't want the data represented through one
visual channel to interfere with the others \citep{Smart2019}. While
this approach works for standard data visualisation, in uncertainty
visualisation it allows the estimate and its variance to be read
separately, preventing us from seeing the single integrated channel.
This problem has actually popped up before in the literature as it has
been repeatedly found that visualising uncertainty and signal separately
causes the uncertainty to simply be ignored \citep{uncertchap2022}.
Additionally, there is research in visual semiotics on mapping
uncertainty to intuitive aesthetic mappings, many of which interfere
with the reading of the estimate such as mapping more uncertain values
to lighter colours \citep{Maceachren2012}. To facilitate a visualisation
of a single integrated value, maybe we should be leveraging
integrability and visual semiotics to create a an ideal situation where
the estimate and uncertainty are separately manipulable but read as a
single channel by the human brain.

The problem is that even if we implement the most extreme versions of
integrabiltiy our methods fall short. The bivariate map in
Figure~\ref{fig-variable} already leveraged the fact that colour hue and
brightness is integrable \citep{Vanderplas2020} and decreasing
saturation should make the colours harder to distinguish, but the signal
is still clearly visible. However, the failure to perform signal
suppression is not the only concern with relying on visual interference.
The first problem is that the saturation relative to the signal is
scaled arbitrarily with little concern for the strength of the signal.
Another issue is that relying on perceptual interference to create
statistical inference may not give us the level of control we need.
Without a strong understanding of how these visual channels collapse
down into a single channel, relying on integrability could create
unintended consequences such as displaying phantom signals or hiding
justified signals. Additionally, multi-dimensional colour palettes can
make the graphics harder to read and hurt the accessibility of the plots
\citep{Vanderplas2015}. This is further complicated by the fact that
human colour perception varies at an individual level. Some women are
believed to have four different cone cells which allows them to perceive
a greater range of colours, while others only have one or two types of
cone cells and have colour deficiencies \citep{simunovic2010}. If this
method were to work, we would need to calibrate each plot to an
individual's ability to perceive colour. Instead of hoping that
uncertainty might collapse signal values into a single dimension, we can
do some of that work ourselves. As a matter of fact, some uncertainty
visualisation authors already have.

\subsubsection{Another example: mapping combined
variables}\label{another-example-mapping-combined-variables}

The Value Suppressing Uncertainty Palette (VSUP) \citep{Correll2018},
was designed with the intention of preventing high uncertainty values
from being extracted from a map, blending colours together as they
become more uncertain. Figure~\ref{fig-vsup} depicts the Iowa choropleth
map from Figure~\ref{fig-variable}, but instead of the standard
bivariate palette, we have used the VSUP that blends high variance
colours together. Since the palette was designed with the extraction of
individual values in mind and it has only been tested on simple value
extraction tasks \citep{Correll2018} or search tasks \citep{Ndlovu2023},
but we can see that, at least for our example, the high uncertainty
spatial trend has functionally disappeared.

While the VSUP map does have signal suppressions, this colour blending
approach creates a trade off visible signals in the plot. If we only use
a single colour to express each random variable, we will need to decide
which signals we suppress and which we do not. The VSUP in
Figure~\ref{fig-vsup} used the tree based method that was used by
\citet{Correll2018}, but \citet{Kay2019} introduces several other
methods and comments that which hypothesis are suppressed and which are
not largely depends on the methods used in combining the colour palettes
and the level at which the blending occurs. This means that the VSUP map
is not suitable for exploratory data analysis, as we need to know the
hypothesis we want to suppress before even generating the map.

\subsubsection{Uncertainty and exploratory data
analysis}\label{uncertainty-and-exploratory-data-analysis}

We often think of visualisations as having two roles in our analysis.
They can simultaneously be used for exploratory data analysis (EDA),
which is the process of searching for interesting hypotheses, and for
confirmatory data analysis (CDA), the process of verifying a hypothesis
\citep{Tukey1977}. Some have argued that the two methods are not
entirely distinct, as it is often difficult to draw a hard line between
EDA and CDA \citep{Hullman2021}. As with many concepts these approaches
exist on a continuum, where we have an inherent trade-off between the
number of hypotheses we can look for and the certainty of any
conclusions reached. It can help to think of the knowledge-generating
process of EDA and CDA as the nozzel on a hose with multiple spray
options, where EDA is a fine misting spray that touches everything in
the room, and CDA is a high-pressure jet capable of obliterating any and
all debris from any single spot.

The EDA versus CDA dichotomy can be compared to the dichotomy between
induction, for building theories, and deduction, for testing theories,
from formal logic. One of the trade off's in the two methods is that
conclusions deduction provide certainty, while conclusions from
induction are inherently uncertain. This means that EDA, the inductive
counterpart, has uncertainty in any conclusions reached through the
method, with the requirement to follow up our new-found hypothesis with
CDA if we want true certainty. This aligns with our earlier definition
of uncertainty as descriptive statistics, one of the standard methods
for EDA, have no inherent uncertainty as they do not look beyond the
data.

This is why, despite the fact that an uncertainty visualisation system
for EDA is one of the most discussed topics in the field
\citep{Hadjimichael2024, geointerviews, MacEachren2005, Sarma2024, Griethe2006},
we have avoided the topic until now. Discussing EDA versus CDA gives us
an additional layer of confusion, as authors frequently conflate the
uncertainty in our data with the uncertainty in our conclusions that is
inherent to the method. Authors often try to account for this inherent
uncertainty and believe it should be incorporated into their plots, an
argument made explicitly by \citet{Hullman2021} when they said there is
no such thing as a ``model-free'' visualisation. The fear of this
inherent uncertainty leads authors to try to perform some kind of
statistical super-test, where they pre-emptively hedge against every
false inference that could possibly be drawn from a graphic
\citep{Guo2024, Savvides2019, Hullman2021}. This is typically done by
visualising a null distribution alongside the observed data on a single
plot, an approach that is statistically identical to the line-up
protocol \citep{Buja2009}. As a line-up protocols were designed to a
graphical parallel to standard statistical hypothesis tests, a core tool
of inductive statistics, they are deductive reasoning, and therefore not
EDA. The sum total of a lot of CDA is not EDA, just as frantically
swinging a high-pressure jet around a room is not equivalent to using a
misting spray. Even if you do manage to wet everything, you are going to
use an exorbitant amount of water to do it (and possibly also break a
few things). This is not to say there is anything wrong with these
approaches; they are incredibly useful tools for induction. The line-up
protocol in particular is a useful alternative to hypothesis testing, as
it allows for statistical inference when there is no classical test
available or when tests involve multiple effects \citep{Hofmann2012}.
Rather, the problem is that we cannot simultaneously perform EDA and
inference as the approaches are, philosophically speaking, perpendicular
to one another.

To truly create an uncertainty visualisation approach that is capable of
EDA, we need to accept that uncertainty is inherent to the method, and
it cannot be pre-emptively removed from the visualisations. If we accept
this fact, then the only possible source of uncertainty in our
``flexible uncertainty visualisation system'' is from the data itself.
That is to say, the only possible way for there to be uncertainty in a
visualisation designed for EDA is that the data itself represents
inference that has been done earlier in our analysis. We can see this in
the explanation of Figure~\ref{fig-ignore} where the uncertainty in all
cases, even measurement error, represents inference that was performed
earlier in our analysis. This does not seem to be well understood in the
uncertainty visualisation literature, where we see authors treat
sampling and prediction distributions as though they are interchangeable
\citep{Hofman2020}, despite the fact that they represent fundamentally
different data.

Unlike in EDA, the signal suppression for VSUP is tied to our
conclusions and not the data itself. Our ability to arbitrarily decide
which values to blend at, or which suppression approach to use means
that the ``uncertainty'' we are visualising will be informed by the
conclusions we are drawing, and not a product of the data itself.
Visualising the uncertainty in this way requires us to have already
decided what signals we will suppress and which we will keep visible.
This leads us to an additional, EDA based, requirement of our
uncertainty visualisation system; the uncertainty and estimate need to
be seen as a single unit, and any signal suppression should be informed
by the data itself and should work the same regardless of any higher
level hypothesis we might generate from the visualisation.

value or pair of values, why don't we just visualise the whole
distribution? This is also a common sentiment in the literature, and we
can see the approach in computer science with the HOPs
\citep{Hullman2015}, in geoscience with pixel-maps
\citep{Lucchesi2021, Blenkinsop2000}, and in statistics with spaghetti

\subsubsection{Quantified versus unquantified
uncertainty}\label{quantified-versus-unquantified-uncertainty}

The visualisation in Figure~\ref{fig-distribution} performs signal
suppression, without sacrificing the viewers ability to extract general
statistics, unless those statistics \emph{should} be harder to extract
due to the uncertainty in the value. By showing our distribution as
``data'' we are able to read the ``uncertainty'' plots using the same
perceptual tasks that are used to read the ``non-uncertainty'' plot.
This is also easier on the reader, as \citet{Ndlovu2023} found that
people read complicated bivariate and VSUP plots the same way they read
choropleth maps, even it is invalid. This approach also does not
significantly hinder our ability to extract the individual values mapped
by the previous plots, as extracting global statistics from a sample can
be done with relative ease \citep{Franconeri2021}. The success of this
approach highlights an interesting problem for uncertainty
visualisation. We are able to include more information by offloading
more computation to visual processing, but should we offload \emph{all}
computations to the visual processing and just visualise the raw data?

This is, in a way, a question about how much of our uncertainty should
be quantified. Quantified uncertainty usually focus narrowly on concepts
such as probability, confidence intervals, variance, error, or precision
\citep{Hullman2018, Maceachren2012, Thomson2005}, while unquantified
uncertainty often includes a broader range of concepts like missing
values, reliability, model validity, or source integrity
\citep{Griethe2006, Leland2005, Pang1997, Pham2009, Boukhelifa2017}.
When discussing uncertainty we typically include these unquantified
uncertainties, not because these things \emph{are} uncertainty, but
because they can \emph{create} uncertainty when we perform inference by
violating the assumption of the uniformity of nature.

If the assumption violations are relevant, and we have the tools to
visualise them, we should. For example, in the event of missing data, we
can use the \texttt{naniar} package \citet{Tierney2023} to include them
as a ``shadow'' in our visualisations to check if missing values have
structure. This approach amounts to just ``showing the data'' which is a
simple but effective option for uncertainty visualisation that is
largely overlooked. While this approach is easy (when the tools for it
exist) this approach will not eliminate trends that have become invalid
due to structure in missing data or an invalid model. We can only
integrate uncertainty as noise when that uncertainty has been
\emph{quantified} as an effect on the estimates we are visualising. This
is not to say you should do one method over another, visualising both
quantified and unquantified uncertainty is necessary for a healthy
analysis. Data analysis often works in cycles, where we find assumption
violations using EDA, quantifying the effect of these violations on our
inference, and then visualising the output of that inference using
uncertainty visualisation to perform even more EDA. Rather, our point is
that uncertainty is a living breathing component of our visualisations
that is always changing, and we should shine a light on it's shape with
visualisation whenever we get the chance.

\section{Evaluating uncertainty
visualisations}\label{evaluating-uncertainty-visualisations}

If we want to make conclusions about how effective any uncertainty
visualisation method is we need to look at the results of evaluation
experiments. Unfortunately, despite the abundance of these studies,
existing literature reviews have struggled to synthesise them into any
common rules \citep{MacEachren2005, Kinkeldey2014, Hullman2016}. This
issue is particular to uncertainty visualisation, and pervasive, so it
begs the question, what is causing it?

There are as many explanations for the noisy evaluation studies, as
there are contradictions in the research itself. \citet{Kim2019}
believes there is some interference from participants prior beliefs;
\citet{Hullman2016} believes the noise in the literature could come from
visual heuristics, subjective probabilities, unknown participant utility
functions, or misunderstanding of statistical concepts (such as
confidence intervals). \citet{Kinkeldey2014} suggest the best
visualisation changes depending on the audience, so we cannot expect the
same results between different subpopulations. \citet{Brennen2018}
attributes issues to cognitive load from complicated uncertainty
visualisations, and the participants prior experience in a topic,
suggesting it is aleviated by measuring confidence and cognative load.
While these issues will certainly have some impact on our ability to
create a cohesive theory, most of them are not unique to uncertainty,
visualisation and fail to explain the problems in this field in
particular. Rather, the issue is likely coming from a disconnection
between the evaluation method and wider goal of the field.

\subsection{Current evaluation
methods}\label{current-evaluation-methods}

The inconsistency in the motivations for uncertainty visualisation bleed
into the evaluation of the methods. Treating the results of these
papers, as though they are somehow agnostic towards their stated
purpose, leads to regularly conflicting results in the field. This
problem is pervasive within standard visualisation evaluation
literature, where studies are regularly criticised for failing to
correctly align questions and plot types \citep{Vanderplas2020} as
though the goals of our analysis and the plots we use are somehow
independent. This becomes particularly egregious in uncertainty
visualisation because, unlike other statistics, uncertainty plays two,
opposite, roles in our analysis. The role of signal is to be visible,
while the role of noise is to obfuscate, so flipping the role of
uncertainty also flips our conclusions about a methods effectiveness.
This problem was also noticed by \citet{Brennen2018}, however their
solution of measuring cognitive load and confidence would not alleviate
this deeper problem. This issue is further aggravated by the fact that
authors often provide little justification for their chosen response
models \citep{Hullman2016}, and the fact that visualisations are
compared on the basis of being ``uncertainty visualisations''
\citep{Ibrekk1987, Hullman2015, Hofman2020}, a classification that holds
little to no meaning. This disregard for the wider motivation of the
field leads to findings that are confusing and conflicting, which cannot
be coalesced into a cohesive theory.

Uncertainty visualisations are most commonly evaluated based on how
accurately viewers can extract an estimate and its variance
\citep{Kinkeldey2014, Hullman2019}. This is not unusual, as direct
observation is the simplest way to verify that information can be
accurately read from a graph \citep{Vanderplas2020}. Unfortunately, this
approach doesn't work for uncertainty visualisation. The second we ask a
specific question about a statistic, that statistic becomes our
inferential statistic, even if the plot was not the intent behind the
plot. By shifting the focus from \(\hat{X}\) to \(Var(\hat{X})\) or
\(P(\hat{X}<x)\) we end up evaluating visualisations on their ability to
convey uncertainty statistics, rather than their ability to perform
signal suppression. There is nothing unique to uncertainty in these
studies, so when we boil the findings down to generalised results, they
simply restate existing principles within information visualisation,
even if they do not realise it themselves. Some of the findings are
particularly obvious, such studies that found participants were more
accurate when reading a probability expressed as a text than when they
had to extract if from a graphic \citep{Cheong2016, Savelli2013}. Other
studies replicate existing research, such as the finding that a
probability mapped to a position is more accurate than one mapped to
area \citep{Ibrekk1987, Gschwandtnei2016}, a finding that follows the
hierarchy of perceptual tasks, which has been established for over 40
years \citep{Cleveland1984}. This extends beyond simple accuracy
evaluations, as we found that colour was more effective than size when
searching for maximums and minimums in variances \citep{Sanyal2009},
even though we have known pre-attentive aesthetics such as colour are
more efficient for search tasks since the 1980s \citep{Vanderplas2020}.
By classifying these studies as evaluations of ``uncertainty
visualisation'' while evaluating uncertainty as signal, we are
encouraged to see successful examples of signal-suppression as failure.
This approach leads authors advise against particular aesthetic mappings
for uncertainty, because it leads to participants have more difficulty
extracting values \citep{Blenkinsop2000}. This conclusion is
antithetical to the goals of signal-suppression and occurs because these
methods evaluate uncertainty as a signal, not as noise.

If we cannot directly measure uncertainty for fear that it turns into
signal, we might then assume we can measure the secondary benefits of
increased transparency. This seems to be the approach of many
visualisation authors, as secondary benefits such as trust, confidence,
and risk aversion are all frequently used in uncertainty evaluation
studies \citep{Hullman2019}. Unfortunately, measuring these secondary
effects often leads to confusing conclusions that simultaneously argue
for, and against, the inclusion of uncertainty. This is most commonly
noticed in the use of trust as a measure, as several authors have
commented that measuring trust, and not transparency, can lead to a
questionable subtext that argues against transparency
\citep{Spiegelhalter2017, ONeill2018}. Confidence also has the
confounding factor that there are many reasons for decreased confidence,
including a poorly constructed visualisation, and using it as an
evaluation metric collapses these sources down into a single metric.
This subtext pops up in the uncertainty visualisation literature where
visualisation authors will opt to leave out uncertainty information
because it decreases confidence in the conclusions \citep{Hullman2020a}.
A sentiment that carries over into evaluations of uncertainty
visualisation, where \citet{Blenkinsop2000} commented that visually
integrable depictions of uncertainty should be avoided, as they decrease
the viewers confidence in their extracted data values. Another secondary
effect that is similar to trust and confidence is risk aversion, which
is an economics term used to describe an agent who would chose a random
variable with a lower expected payout because it also has a lower
variance. Risk aversion's role in the uncertainty visualisation is, to
be frank, confusing, as it is unclear whether we shoud want more
rational decisions that ignore uncertainty, or less rational decisions
that incorporate uncertainty. The uncertainty visualisation literature
does not clear this up as authors will argue that more risk aversion is
better in one paper \citep{Hullman2019}, and argue for less risk
aversion (by proxy of suggesting rational agents as a benchmark) in the
next \citep{wu2023rational}. Ultimately, these approaches have similar
issues to the value extraction studies, except they are slightly more
confusing in their goals leading them to simultaneously argue for an
against the inclusion of uncertainty in a visualisation.

This is made even more complicated by the fact that there is no standard
technique to evaluate uncertainty as noise, and attempts to do so are
often ad hoc with varying levels of success. Some authors try to
incorporate including uncertainty by asking vague questions but compare
the results to a ground truth, creating a situation where plots are
\emph{evaluated} as a value extraction study without participants being
asked the \emph{questions} of a value extraction task. Examples include
asking participants for the ``best estimate'' \citep{Ibrekk1987}, or
identify when a distribution is ``furthest to the right''
\citep{Hofmann2012} and then compare these results to the distributions
expected value. The convoluted questions designed to incorporate
uncertainty information not only lead to noisy results, but frequently
confuse the participants and lead to obviously wrong responses. For
example, \citet{Hullman2015} asked participants to compare two groups, A
and B, and had many participants say that group A was more likely to be
bigger, despite group B having a higher mean. In another study
\citet{Gschwandtnei2016} ask participants the ``probability that the
interval has already ended at the marked point in time?'' and
participants replied with the probability that the interval had already
started. The confusion from trying to incorporate uncertainty
uncertainty can extend to the authors of a study, who often tie
themselves in knots trying to come up with measurements for the effects
of noise. We can see an example of this in the two \citet{Padilla2017}
where the first study required participants to assume that an oil rig
being ``more likely to be hit'' by a hurricane would \emph{not}
translate to the rig sustaining ``more damage'', while the second
required participants to assume the opposite. This clearly contradictory
assumption in how to appropriately use uncertainty information seemed to
go unnoticed by the authors. While these approaches are certainly a step
in the right direction, the experiments overcomplicate the problem and
produce results that are not even consistent within the individual
experiment.

This is not to say all all evaluation studies fail to properly evaluate
uncertainty as noise. There are several studies that ask participants to
identify a particular signal that the noise is trying to obfuscate
\citep{Kale2018, Correll2014} which seems to be an effective method. The
only problem with these studies is that most uncertainty visualisation
methods are not integrated into \emph{the grammar of graphics}
\citep{Leland2005, ggplot2} so we regularly see comparisons between
disparate plots that that would never be considered substitutes for one
another outside the artificial ``uncertainty visualisation'' framework
they are placed within. For example \citet{Kale2018} compared static bar
charts with error bars to a bar chart with animated samples, meaning
that any difference in participants ability to read the plot could be
due to the statistic (confidence interval versus sample), the geometry
(bars versus intervals), or the use of animation (static versuss
animated plots). This means that even when evaluations \emph{are} done
correctly, there is no generalisable theory we can take from the
results.

The point here is not to mock the existing literature for poor academic
merit. If you were to go read these papers yourself, you would find them
to (usually) be logically consistent and well formulated pieces of work.
Rather, the point is to illustrate that evaluating uncertainty as a
noise is surprisingly difficult. Designing tests for signal suppression
will require a formalisation of uncertainty within the grammar of
graphics, as well as improved evaluation methods.

\subsection{Implicit Hypotehsis
Testing}\label{implicit-hypotehsis-testing}

The main problems with current uncertainty visualisation evaluations is
that they often require explicit (or convoluted) questions about the
variance. Asking direct questions about the statistics or outcomes is
not an explicit requirement of visualisation evaluations. In their
review of testing statistical graphics, \citet{Vanderplas2020} drew a
distinction between explicit tests, where participants are asked direct
questions about specific features of a plot, and implicit testing, where
users identify both the purpose and function of the plot. The lineup
protocol is the most salient example of the implicit approach. Lineups
are a confirmatory visualisation tool where participants are shown a set
of \(M-1\) plots, and asked to identify the plot that is the ``most
different'', leaving participants to decide what ``most different''
means to them, even if it is not what the authors intended
\citep{vanderplas2017}. The implicit testing does not limit the
versaility of the approach, with the lineup being used to evaluate the
effectiveness of different types of plots \citep{Hofmann2012}, colour
palettes \citep{Reda2021}, and design decisions \citep{vanderplas2017}.

Lineup protocols are not only a useful for their implicit testing, but
they also have parallels to hypothesis testing that can be leveraged for
uncertainty visualisation. The concept of signal suppression is, at its
core, an assertion of statistical validity, which means the visibility
of signals should be directly proportional to p-values or some
equivalent measure. This comparison is not new in uncertainty
visualisation where parallels have been drawn to frequentist statistics
by \citet{Correll2014} who compared results to Cohen's D, and to
Bayesian statistics by \citet{Kim2019} who evaluated plots based on
their impact on users prior beliefs. The comparison to hypothesis
testing is far more natural for the lineup protocol, with
\citet{Majumder2013} explicitly defines a visual test statistic, while
\citet{Patrick2023} compared standard statistical tests to the
lineup-protocol using power curves. The parallels to line-ups and
uncertainty visualisation are numerous, with the relationship even being
identified by \citet{Hullman2015} in their development of hypothetical
outcome plots.

The lineup protocol and uncertainty visualisation share a lot in common,
with the motivation behind lineups also rooted in checking if perceived
patterns are real or merely the result of chance
\citep{Buja2009, Wickham2010}. As both approaches are attempting to do
the same thing, it is likely that we are unable to leverage the lineup
protocol directly to evaluate uncertainty visualisation, but new
evaluation methodology should try to learn from the success of the
lineup approach. Designing an implicit testing method for uncertainty
visualisation that allows us to draw parallels to standard notions of
statistical significance, would solve many of the issues with the
current evaluation approaches.

\subsection{Heuristics research}\label{heuristics-research}

There is one element of uncertainty visualisation research that we have
not touched on yet, but is incredibly consistent, and that is the
research around visual heuristics. The uncertainty visualisation
heuristics research, unlike the plot evaluation work, is increbidly
consistent, likely because heuristics are indifferent to the goal of our
analysis and uncertainty's role as signal or noise. Several heuristics
are of particular importance for uncertainty visualisation. We will give
a brief overview of them here, and for a more in depth analysis, we
recommend reading \citet{uncertchap2022} as they provide a more in depth
review.

The use of confidence intervals or boundaries have a few known issues in
their interpretation. The sine illusion can cause the confidence
interval of a smoothed sine curve to seem wider at the peaks than the
troughs, causing us to underestimate uncertainty associated with
changing values \citep{Vanderplas2015}. Similarly researchers frequently
find that visual boundaries such as the cone of uncertainty in hurricane
forcasts \citep{uncertchap2022}, or confidence bands or ellipses
\citep{vanderplas2017} create the impression of a meaningful bound,
separating points outside the bounds from those within it which can
create problems if the bound is not inherently meaningful.

While many researchers tend to prefer frequency framing, they are not
without their critiques. Points that were on an outcome of an ensemble
display were perceived as more likely than points not on an outcome,
even when the point that was not on an outcome was closer to the centre
of the distribution (and therefore more likely) \citep{Padilla2017}.
This is considered to be a variation of the within bar bias, where
outcomes within the bar on a bar chart as percieved to be more likely
than those outside it \citep{Newman2012}.

We should also try and avoid visualsing central estimates if possible as
several studies have found that viewers ignore uncertainty and compare
the difference between the two means if they have the option
\citep{uncertchap2022}.

These heuristics have the potential to create noise in signal
suppression evaluations, and should be kept in mind when designing
uncertainty visualisations as well as evaluation experiments.

\section{Conclusions and Future work}\label{conclusions-and-future-work}

This paper has identified gaps in the uncertainty visualisation
literature that must be filled for the field to progress. We formalised
the uncertainty visualisation problem, and in doing so highlighted an
untouched area of uncertainty visualisation research.

\emph{Experimental practices on uncertainty visualisation need to be
standardised.} Uncertainty visualisations for decision making treat
uncertainty as signal, while visualisations for signal-suppression treat
uncertainty as noise. As the literature currently exists, there is no
way to combine papers to get a meaningful sense of how uncertainty
information is understood by a viewer. Researchers need to ensure that
when they identify the motivation behind their visualisation technique,
and that their evaluation methods match the motivations of the paper.
Additionally \emph{experimental methods that evaluate uncertainty as
noise need to be developed.}

\emph{Experimenters should consider evaluating visual aesthetics on how
well they suppress information in a graphic.} Research into separability
and integrability is of particular interest to uncertainty
visualisation, as it allows interference from the uncertainty variable.
When designing experiments, authors often choose aesthetics that are
visually distinguishable; uncertainty visualisation authors should
consider doing the opposite.

\emph{Software that allows users to easily perform signal suppression
should be developed.} Existing uncertainty visualisation methods view a
distribution as its own object and there are no software options for the
``an uncertainty visualisation is a function of an existing
visualisation'' philosophy.

Signal suppression is an untouched area of visualisation research and
developing methods for the practice may require us to challenge our
entire notion of what makes a good visualisation. We are interested to
see how this challenge plays out.
